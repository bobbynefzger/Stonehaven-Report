Site / structural / system scope appears underpriced

Observation

Unusual construction-specific risk identified

Site is on private septic with no public sewer; approved permit specifies a 2,000-gal tank + dose tank + 852 LF absorption field on an 8.4-acre sloped…

Observation
The project depends entirely on a privately engineered on-site sewage management system sized for a 5-bedroom, 4.5-bath home with an in-law suite above the garage. The septic permit imposes 19 installation conditions, including precise absorption field placement in undisturbed Appling soils, surface-water diversion requirements, and a prohibition on landscaping before final inspection — any deviation voids the permit. The basement plumbing outlet level and the sloped site (8–10 ft rear grade drop) introduce additional complexity for the force-main routing from the dose tank to the distribution box, and the permit explicitly states work must stop if encountered soils differ from the soil report.
Source
Permit + Appraisal + Description of materials + Plans
Recommend review: Review whether the septic system installation plan — including force-main routing from the basement plumbing outlet, absorption field staking in undisturbed Appling soils, and all 19 permit conditions — has been confirmed by the engineer of record as feasible given the sloped site and walkout basement configuration.

Appraisal assumptions differ from the construction documents

Appraisal shows 4,305 SF / 4 bed / 3.5 bath; plans show 4,886 SF / 5 bed / 4.5 bath — a 581 SF GLA gap and one extra bedroom and bath.

Observation
The plans show a total heated area of 4,886 SF across the main floor and the in-law suite above the garage, with 5 bedrooms and 4.5 baths. The appraisal uses 4,305 SF GLA, 4 bedrooms, and 3.5 baths, explicitly treating the 581 SF in-law suite as a separate accessory unit excluded from GLA because it lacks internal access to the main dwelling. This exclusion drives the bedroom and bath shortfall as well: the suite's bedroom and bathroom are not counted in the appraisal's room tally. If the plans ultimately configure interior access between the suite and the main dwelling, the appraisal's GLA and room counts would need to be revised upward; conversely, if permanent separation is confirmed, the appraisal's treatment is consistent with standard GLA methodology but the budget and cost approach would be pricing different finished areas.
Source
Appraisal + Plans
Recommended next step: Confirm with the appraiser whether the final construction configuration will provide or permanently preclude interior access between the in-law suite and the main dwelling, and whether the appraisal's GLA, room count, and value conclusion would change under either scenario.
